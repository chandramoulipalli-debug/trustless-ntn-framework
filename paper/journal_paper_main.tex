\begin{abstract}
Non-Terrestrial Networks (NTNs) comprising Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
satellites, High-Altitude Platform Systems (HAPS), and Unmanned Aerial
Vehicles (UAVs) are integral to emerging Sixth-Generation (6G)
communication architectures, enabling global IoT coverage.
However, the combination of long propagation delays (up to 300\,ms
round-trip for Geostationary Earth Orbit links), intermittent
connectivity, multi-domain administration, and large-scale adversarial
exposure makes conventional centralized trust management infeasible
in NTN environments.
This paper proposes a \emph{trustless-by-design} security framework
that eliminates dependence on any single trusted authority.
The framework integrates four mutually reinforcing components:
a permissioned distributed ledger for immutable trust evidence;
continuous Zero-Trust Architecture (ZTA) authentication with orbital
proofs; privacy-preserving trust aggregation via Paillier additively
homomorphic encryption and differential privacy; and a delay-aware
trust aging model with per-segment exponential decay rates
calibrated to NTN link dynamics.
We present the first quantitative evaluation of this framework
through six experiments on a heterogeneous 1{,}000-node NTN simulation
(30 Monte-Carlo runs each).
The proposed framework achieves a malicious node detection latency of
\textbf{35.9\,$\pm$\,5.6 rounds} (95\,\% CI), representing a
$4.3\times$ and $6.1\times$ improvement over Static Distributed Ledger
Technology and Centralized Trust baselines respectively.
Post-partition trust recovery shows a detection F1 improvement of
$+0.130$ over the pre-partition baseline, confirming ledger
reconciliation effectiveness.
We further derive a closed-form expression for the
segment-optimal detection threshold, reducing the false positive rate
from 26.8\,\% (conservative fixed threshold) to near zero analytically.
Paillier encryption overhead is 755\,ms per 50-contributor
aggregation cycle at 1024-bit key length, within the 60-second round
budget.
Computation time scales as $\mathcal{O}(N)$ from 5.1\,ms at
$N\!=\!100$ nodes to 227.7\,ms at $N\!=\!5{,}000$ nodes.
The complete simulation codebase and experimental results are
released as a reproducible open-source artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ======================================================================

Sixth-Generation (6G) systems are evolving toward fully integrated
space-air-ground architectures that merge terrestrial infrastructure
with NTNs comprising LEO, Medium Earth Orbit (MEO), and Geostationary
Earth Orbit (GEO) satellites, HAPS, and UAVs~\cite{azari2021,
giordani2020}.
This integration promises ubiquitous connectivity, extended coverage
for remote and disaster-affected regions, and support for large-scale
IoT, autonomous transport, and emergency response.
However, the NTN segment introduces security and trust challenges
that differ fundamentally from those encountered in terrestrial
networks.

\subsection{The Trust Problem in 6G NTNs}

NTN links exhibit long and variable propagation delays (GEO round-trip
reaches 250--300\,ms), Doppler shifts, intermittent connectivity,
and frequent handovers spanning multiple administrative domains.
Under these conditions, conventional trust models face three
structural failures.
First, \emph{temporal staleness}: trust evidence generated at time
$t_k$ may not arrive until $t_k + \Delta t$, yet most existing
systems treat trust scores as time-invariant once computed.
Second, \emph{partition exploitation}: when segments lose connectivity,
nodes that misbehave in one domain can maintain built-up reputation
in another.
Third, \emph{centralization failure}: a single trust authority becomes
unreachable during partitions, creating a denial-of-service
vulnerability on the trust plane itself.

These failure modes enable several attacks specific to NTN
environments: \emph{build-then-betray} (accumulate trust during
stable periods, exploit it after handover), \emph{evidence replay}
(resubmit stale high-quality evidence after the originating
interaction becomes unverifiable), \emph{partition-phase Sybil}
(create multiple identities during a disconnected window), and
\emph{on-off oscillation} (alternate good and bad behavior to
avoid sustained detection).

\subsection{Research Gap and Motivation}

Existing approaches address the above threats only partially.
Blockchain-based reputation systems such as NxGenT~\cite{nxgent}
employ monotonic trust accumulation, leaving nodes vulnerable to
build-then-betray attacks.
Zero-trust adaptations for satellite communications~\cite{ztisl}
focus on access control without modeling long-term trust decay under
delay.
UAV-centric federated learning approaches~\cite{uav_fl} introduce
dynamic score adjustment but apply a uniform UAV-class decay rate
to all node types, incorrectly penalizing stable GEO relay nodes.
The Age of Trust (AoT) concept~\cite{aot} formalizes staleness in
zero-trust systems but has not been integrated with distributed
ledgers at the NTN scale.
No prior work simultaneously addresses decentralization, per-segment
delay-aware decay, cryptographic privacy, and NTN partition
resilience in a unified, evaluated architecture.

\subsection{Contributions}

This paper makes the following contributions beyond its prior
conference version~\cite{conf_version}:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Unified trustless framework}: A four-layer
        architecture integrating permissioned DLT, ZTA, Paillier
        homomorphic encryption, differential privacy, and per-segment
        delay-aware trust aging (Sections~\ref{sec:system}--\ref{sec:trust}).

  \item \textbf{First quantitative evaluation}: Six experiments on a
        1{,}000-node heterogeneous NTN simulation with 30 Monte-Carlo
        runs each, producing the first measured detection latency,
        cryptographic overhead, privacy-utility tradeoff, and
        scalability characterization for this class of framework
        (Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}).

  \item \textbf{Segment-optimal threshold calibration}: A closed-form
        derivation of the per-segment detection threshold from the
        steady-state trust equation, resolving the false positive
        penalty incurred by fixed global thresholds
        (Section~\ref{sec:threshold}).

  \item \textbf{Open reproducible artifact}: Full simulation codebase,
        configuration, and result data released under MIT license with
        GitHub Actions CI for independent reproducibility verification
        (Section~\ref{sec:simulation}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Blockchain-Based Trust for 6G and NTNs}

Distributed ledger technology has been widely studied for
decentralized trust management in 6G systems~\cite{kalla2022,
khan2023}.
NxGenT~\cite{nxgent} implements blockchain-based reputation in which
interaction records are stored immutably and trust scores are computed
via smart contracts.
Its trust values accumulate monotonically; a node that turns malicious
retains the reputation built during its compliant phase.
Moreover, NxGenT assumes stable terrestrial connectivity with
low-latency ledger access, which is ill-suited to NTN round-trip
times and intermittent validator availability.
Yang~\textit{et al.}~\cite{yang2024} propose a consensus-integrated
trust scheme for 6G, but similarly do not model long propagation
delays or multi-orbit reconciliation.

\subsection{Zero-Trust Architectures for NTNs}

ZTA has emerged as a foundational 6G security paradigm~\cite{chen2024}.
Pokhrel~\cite{ztisl} adapts ZTA to satellite communication using
orbital parameters for continuous authentication, providing
verifiable session tokens that are hard for attackers to forge.
However, this work concentrates on access decisions without modeling
longer-term trust evolution or supporting trust-score degradation
under sustained misbehavior.
Centralized policy engines assumed in prior ZTA designs become
unreachable during partitions, creating a systemic vulnerability
in the very systems meant to replace centralized trust.

\subsection{UAV-Centric and Federated Learning Approaches}

Recent work pairs blockchain with federated learning for UAV-assisted
NTN trust~\cite{uav_fl}, adding dynamic score adjustment alongside
energy-aware consensus.
This approach demonstrates the value of continuous re-evaluation but
restricts its design to UAV nodes and does not address GEO-scale
delays or cross-orbit reconciliation.
Applying a UAV-class decay rate uniformly to all segment types
over-penalizes stable GEO relay nodes, as we confirm empirically in
Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\subsection{Age of Trust and Staleness-Aware Evaluation}

Xiao~\textit{et al.}~\cite{aot} formalize the Age of Trust (AoT) as
an analogue of Age of Information~\cite{kaul2012} for zero-trust
systems, quantifying how verification evidence grows stale over time.
This concept motivates the per-segment exponential decay in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:aging}, but has not previously been embedded in a
distributed ledger-based NTN framework with measured performance
results.

\subsection{Privacy-Preserving Trust Aggregation}

Homomorphic encryption and differential privacy for trust systems
are studied in~\cite{uav_fl,kalla2022} but without explicit NTN
delay modeling or empirical cryptographic overhead measurements.
The Paillier scheme~\cite{paillier1999} provides additive
homomorphism exactly suited to trust score summation without
exposing individual contributions, and the Gaussian differential
privacy mechanism~\cite{dwork2014} calibrates noise to a privacy
budget $\varepsilon$.
Our work is the first to measure and report Paillier overhead and
privacy-utility tradeoffs in the NTN trust context.

No prior work simultaneously achieves decentralization, strong
privacy, per-segment delay-aware trust aging, full NTN coverage

\section{System Model and Threat Model}
\label{sec:system}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Network Topology}

We consider a heterogeneous 6G NTN comprising three interconnected
segments: (i) a \emph{space segment} of LEO, MEO, and GEO satellites
forming dynamic mesh topologies; (ii) an \emph{air segment} of HAPS
and UAVs serving as relays and aerial access points; and (iii) a
\emph{ground segment} of base stations, gateways, and IoT devices.

Each inter-segment link category exhibits distinct trust dynamics.
LEO-to-ground links undergo rapid handovers with sub-second
disruptions.
GEO-to-ground links exhibit high but stable latency reaching
250--300\,ms round-trip.
UAV-to-UAV links are mobile with highly variable quality.
No centralized authority can maintain coherent, up-to-date trust
across all segments simultaneously, making distributed, delay-aware
trust essential.

\subsection{Trust Evidence Model}

Trust is rooted in observable interactions.
When nodes $i$ and $j$ interact, the encounter generates a trust
evidence record carrying: pseudonymous identifiers, service quality
metrics (delivery ratio, latency, packet loss rate), a timestamp $t_k$,
and a cryptographic signature.
Since evidence generated at $t_k$ may not arrive until $t_k + \Delta t$,
propagation delay is explicitly factored into trust computation.
Records support both direct trust (first-hand observations) and
indirect trust (third-party recommendations), with each type weighted
differently during aggregation to limit the influence of compromised
recommenders.

\subsection{Formal Threat Model}

We assume an adaptive adversary capable of:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Node capture}: compromising satellite, UAV, or ground
        nodes for impersonation and false feedback injection.
  \item \textbf{Delay and replay exploitation}: replaying stale
        high-quality evidence to desynchronize trust views.
  \item \textbf{Partition exploitation}: behaving maliciously in one
        segment while maintaining trust in another.
  \item \textbf{Sybil and identity attacks}: creating multiple
        pseudonymous identities to skew aggregation.
  \item \textbf{On-off attacks}: alternating compliant and malicious
        behavior to avoid sustained detection.
  \item \textbf{Badmouthing}: transmitting falsely low scores about
        legitimate peers to degrade their reputations.
  \item \textbf{Build-then-betray}: behaving compliantly to build
        trust, then misbehaving once reputation is established.
\end{itemize}
We assume that cryptographic primitives remain computationally secure,
that a sufficient subset of nodes participates honestly in permissioned
consensus, and that time synchronization is adequate for event
ordering.

\section{Trustless-by-Design Framework Architecture}
\label{sec:framework}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Design Principles}

Five principles guide the design:
(i) \emph{Elimination of Implicit Trust} — every claim requires
verifiable evidence;
(ii) \emph{Decentralization by Construction} — no centralized server
or authority is assumed;
(iii) \emph{Delay and Disruption Tolerance} — delayed feedback and
network partitions are first-class operating conditions;
(iv) \emph{Privacy Preservation} — sensitive behavioral data stay
protected throughout aggregation;
(v) \emph{Continuous Verification} — trust is updated and degraded
dynamically in line with zero-trust principles.

\subsection{Four-Layer Architecture}

The framework organizes into four vertically stacked layers, as shown
in Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}.

\subsubsection{Layer 1 — Decentralized Ledger of Trust}
A permissioned distributed ledger (blockchain or DAG-based) maintained
by elected NTN nodes stores immutable records of service interactions,
trust-relevant events, and aggregated trust updates.
Each entry includes pseudonymous identifiers, encoded quality metrics,
timestamps, and cryptographic signatures, providing tamper-evidence,
auditability, and non-repudiation.
When network partitions occur, each disconnected component carries on
with its local ledger view; upon reconnection, a reconciliation
protocol merges divergent histories while applying trust aging to
stale records, ensuring that malicious nodes cannot carry over
inflated pre-partition scores.

\subsubsection{Layer 2 — Zero-Trust Authentication}
ZTA serves as the authentication backbone.
Every session requires cryptographic mutual authentication with
freshness checks.
Satellite and aerial nodes strengthen authentication using orbital
parameters, which exhibit predictable spatial-temporal traits that are
hard for attackers to forge.
No implicit trust zones exist; even validator nodes must continuously
authenticate and maintain acceptable trust levels.
Failed authentication attempts immediately trigger trust score
penalties and are recorded on the ledger, creating an auditable trail
of security incidents.

\subsubsection{Layer 3 — Trust Evaluation and Aging}
The trust evaluation pipeline follows five steps:
(1) nodes monitor peer behavior and generate signed local evidence;
(2) incoming evidence is verified for authenticity, integrity, and
freshness;
(3) valid evidence is aggregated via privacy-preserving mechanisms;
(4) trust scores are updated via the delay-aware aging model
(Section~\ref{sec:trust});
(5) updated aggregates are committed to the ledger through smart
contracts.

\subsubsection{Layer 4 — Policy and Decision Enforcement}
Smart contracts enforce threshold-based access control for routing
and relaying decisions.
Policies specify minimum trust thresholds per service type, eligibility
requirements for validator roles, and conditions for cross-domain
roaming.
All access decisions are recorded for auditability.

\section{Privacy-Preserving and Delay-Aware Trust Modeling}
\label{sec:trust}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Pseudonymous Identities and Homomorphic Aggregation}

Nodes interact under pseudonymous cryptographic identifiers rotated
periodically.
All ledger entries reference only these pseudonyms, limiting
eavesdropper inference while preserving legal accountability.
To prevent individual feedback from being exposed, node $i$'s
feedback $f_{ij}(t)$ about peer $j$ is encrypted using the Paillier
additively homomorphic cryptosystem~\cite{paillier1999}:
%
\begin{equation}
  c_{ij}(t) = \mathrm{Enc}\!\left(f_{ij}(t)\right). \label{eq:enc}
\end{equation}
%
Smart contracts compute the aggregate ciphertext:
%
\begin{equation}
  C_j(t) = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{N}_j(t)} c_{kj}(t), \label{eq:agg}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{N}_j(t)$ is the contributor set at time $t$.
A threshold committee jointly decrypts $C_j(t)$, keeping individual
contributions confidential while preserving verifiability.
This mechanism implements Eqs.~\eqref{eq:enc}--\eqref{eq:agg}
exactly: the smart contract never accesses individual $c_{kj}$
values, and the decryption committee requires a threshold quorum to
prevent any single node from learning the disaggregated feedback.

\subsection{Differential Privacy for Aggregate Queries}

To prevent inference attacks from repeated aggregate queries,
differential privacy (DP) is applied to published trust statistics:
%
\begin{equation}
  Q'(\mathcal{D}) = Q(\mathcal{D}) + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2),
  \label{eq:dp}
\end{equation}
%
where $\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$ denotes zero-mean Gaussian noise and
$\sigma$ is calibrated as:
%
\begin{equation}
  \sigma = \frac{\Delta_f \sqrt{2 \ln(1.25/\delta)}}{\varepsilon},
  \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}
%
with $\Delta_f$ the query sensitivity, $\varepsilon$ the privacy
budget, and $\delta$ the failure probability.
This guarantees $(\varepsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy~\cite{dwork2014},
ensuring that the inclusion or exclusion of any single node's trust
record does not significantly affect the published result.

\subsection{Delay-Aware Trust Aging Model}
\label{sec:aging}

The core technical contribution is the delay-aware trust aging
mechanism.
Let $T_{ij}(t) \in [0,1]$ denote the trust score maintained by node
$i$ for peer $j$ at time $t$.
The update rule is:
%
\begin{equation}
  T_{ij}(t+\Delta t) =
    \alpha \cdot T_{ij}(t) \cdot e^{-\lambda_s \Delta t}
    + (1-\alpha)\,S_{ij}(t),
  \label{eq:aging}
\end{equation}
%
where $\alpha \in (0,1)$ weights historical trust against recent
evidence (set to $\alpha=0.7$), $\lambda_s > 0$ is a per-segment
decay rate, $\Delta t$ captures the elapsed time including
communication delay ($\Delta t = T_{\mathrm{round}} + d_{ij}/1000$
in seconds, with $d_{ij}$ the link delay in milliseconds), and
$S_{ij}(t) \in [0,1]$ reflects the latest interaction quality.
The score is bounded to $[0,1]$ after each update.

The segment-specific decay rates are calibrated to NTN link dynamics
(Table~\ref{tab:lambda}): UAV links ($\lambda_s = 0.05$\,s$^{-1}$)
decay most aggressively because mobility makes trust evidence stale
quickly; GEO links ($\lambda_s = 0.005$\,s$^{-1}$) decay slowly
because high but stable latency means evidence remains relatively
fresh across update intervals; LEO and ground values fall between.

          $\bar{s}_{\mathrm{mal}}=0.10$)}
\label{tab:lambda}

The exponential decay provides three security properties:
(i) resistance to build-then-betray attacks, since trust erodes
unless actively reinforced by fresh positive evidence;
(ii) automatic discount of stale evidence in proportion to age,
without requiring explicit negative feedback;
(iii) natural trust degradation during partitions, preventing
pre-partition scores from being exploited upon reconnection.

\section{Simulation Methodology}
\label{sec:simulation}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Topology and Node Population}

We simulate a heterogeneous NTN graph $G=(V,E)$ with $N=1{,}000$
nodes distributed across six segment types: 2 GEO, 4 MEO, 30 LEO,
10 HAPS, 50 UAV, and 904 ground IoT nodes.
Links are generated by a segment-aware random graph model with
per-link propagation delays drawn uniformly from segment-typical
ranges.
Connectivity is guaranteed via a minimum spanning-tree augmentation
step.
All topology parameters are fixed in the published configuration
file to ensure byte-for-byte reproducibility.

\subsection{Simulation Protocol}

Each simulation consists of $R=500$ rounds of duration
$T_{\mathrm{round}}=60$\,s, representing one NTN interaction cycle
consistent with 3GPP NR-NTN scheduling periodicity for IoT
devices.
In each round, every node $i$ observes one randomly selected
neighbour $j$ and computes a feedback score based on packet-delivery
ratio and authentication success.
The effective time step is
$\Delta t = T_{\mathrm{round}} + d_{ij}/1000$ seconds, where
$d_{ij}$ is the link delay in milliseconds.
Initial trust scores are set to $T_{ij}(0) = 0.5$ for all pairs.

\subsection{Baseline Models}

Five trust models are evaluated:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Proposed}: Eq.~\eqref{eq:aging} with per-segment
        $\lambda_s$, Paillier HE aggregation, simulated PBFT-like
        DLT consensus, and ZTA policy layer.
  \item \textbf{Centralized Trust}: A single authority aggregates all
        feedback; unreachable during partition phases.
  \item \textbf{Static DLT (NxGenT)~\cite{nxgent}}: Permissioned DLT
        with monotonic trust accumulation; no temporal decay.
  \item \textbf{ZT Auth-Only~\cite{ztisl}}: Binary ZTA authentication
        without a continuous trust score.
  \item \textbf{UAV+FL Blockchain~\cite{uav_fl}}: Federated-learning
        trust aggregation with uniform $\lambda_{\mathrm{UAV}}=0.05$
        decay applied to all segment types.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Attack Scenarios}

A fraction $f \in \{5\%,10\%,15\%,20\%,25\%,30\%\}$ of nodes are
designated malicious, with roles drawn uniformly from:
\emph{on-off} (alternating compliant/malicious every 50 rounds);
\emph{build-then-betray} (compliant for rounds 1--150, malicious
thereafter);
\emph{Sybil} (colluding group with coordinated false feedback); and
\emph{badmouthing} (transmits falsely low scores for legitimate peers).
Feedback scores for malicious nodes are
$S_{ij}(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0.10, 0.05^2)$ clipped to $[0,0.15]$
during attack phases and $S_{ij}(t) \sim \mathcal{U}(0.70, 1.00)$
during compliant phases; legitimate nodes produce
$S_{ij}(t) \sim \mathcal{U}(0.70, 1.00)$.
Partition experiments inject a 50-round partition disconnecting
approximately 30\,\% of nodes.

\subsection{Statistical Protocol and Reproducibility}

All experiments use global seed 42 with per-run offset $r \in
\{0,\ldots,29\}$ across 30 independent Monte-Carlo trials, reported
as mean\,$\pm$\,95\,\% CI ($1.96 \cdot \hat{\sigma}/\!\sqrt{30}$).
The full codebase is available at the URL given in the author block.
A GitHub Actions CI workflow re-executes 16 unit tests on every
commit, verifying trust boundedness, monotone decay ordering across
segments, and Paillier encryption/decryption correctness.

\section{Quantitative Performance Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Trust Dynamics and Detection Latency}

\subsubsection{Setup}
$N=1{,}000$ nodes, $f=20\%$ malicious, 500 rounds, 30 runs.
Detection latency is defined as the number of rounds from attack
onset until the malicious node's trust score remains continuously
below threshold $\theta = 0.40$ for at least three consecutive rounds,
averaged over all malicious nodes and all 30 runs.

\subsubsection{Results}
Table~\ref{tab:comparison} and Fig.~\ref{fig:detection} report the
results.
The proposed framework detects malicious nodes within
\textbf{35.9\,$\pm$\,5.6 rounds}, achieving a
$\mathbf{4.3\times}$ speedup over Static~DLT (152.6\,$\pm$\,12.8)
and a $\mathbf{6.1\times}$ speedup over Centralized~Trust
(219.8\,$\pm$\,16.3).
The Centralized~Trust model's high latency reflects its inability to
update trust scores during partition events, as the authority becomes
unreachable.
Static~DLT's latency reflects the monotonic accumulation property:
a build-then-betray attacker retains its accumulated reputation for
many rounds after betrayal begins.
ZT~Auth-Only (85.5\,$\pm$\,7.8 rounds) improves over centralized and
static DLT by using continuous re-authentication, but cannot signal
\emph{gradual} trust erosion; detection requires the node to fail
authentication entirely.

The UAV+FL baseline achieves the lowest latency (17.3\,$\pm$\,4.0
rounds) by applying the aggressive UAV decay rate ($\lambda=0.05$)
uniformly to all node types.
As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:threshold}, this comes at the cost
of a false positive rate of 27.9\,\%, because the uniform aggressive
decay also depresses the trust of legitimate GEO nodes that should
retain stable scores.
The proposed model correctly distinguishes fast-moving UAVs from
stable GEO satellites, trading 18.6 additional detection rounds for
a qualitatively different false-alarm characteristic.

At round~500, the proposed framework's detection F1 is
$0.236\,\pm\,0.011$ (Exp.~1; 30-run mean~$\pm$~95\,\%~CI),
consistent with $0.225\,\pm\,0.015$ measured independently in
the Exp.~6 sweep at $f\!=\!20\,\%$.
This represents a $\mathbf{2.8\times}$ improvement over
Centralized~Trust ($0.080\,\pm\,0.012$) and a $1.6\times$
improvement over Static~DLT ($0.141\,\pm\,0.017$), as also reported
in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}.

\subsubsection{Interpretation}
Detection latency is the primary performance metric for NTN trust
frameworks.
Single-snapshot F1 and AUC-ROC undercount framework effectiveness
because on-off attackers deliberately maintain compliant behavior
during observation windows; at any fixed snapshot, they are
indistinguishable from legitimate nodes regardless of the model.
Detection latency, which integrates temporal trust evolution, is
not susceptible to this evasion strategy and more accurately
reflects the framework's real-time response capability.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Partition Resilience}

\subsubsection{Setup}
A 50-round network partition disconnects approximately 30\,\% of
nodes beginning at round 200.
Detection F1 is measured immediately before partition onset
(pre-partition) and 50 rounds after reconnection (post-partition).

\subsubsection{Results}
The proposed framework's detection F1 \emph{improves} from
$0.202$ (pre-partition) to $0.332$ (post-partition), a
$\mathbf{+0.130}$ gain (Fig.~\ref{fig:partition}).
This counter-intuitive improvement is mechanistically correct:
during the partition, trust aging accumulates penalties against
malicious nodes that had previously built reputation.
Upon reconnection, the reconciliation protocol applies trust aging
to stale partition-era records, meaning that nodes whose scores
decayed below $\theta$ during isolation are treated with appropriate
caution rather than being re-admitted on pre-partition credentials.
The mean GEO link trust after partition is $0.108$ and mean LEO
link trust is $0.110$ for malicious nodes, confirming that the decay
mechanism effectively erodes reputation during disconnected periods.

\subsection{Experiment 6: Attack Resilience Across Malicious Fractions}

Fig.~\ref{fig:attack} plots detection F1 and AUC-ROC for all five
models as the malicious node fraction varies from 5\,\% to 30\,\%.

The proposed framework's F1 increases monotonically from
$0.086\,\pm\,0.011$ (5\,\% malicious) to $0.286\,\pm\,0.014$
(30\,\% malicious).
Centralized Trust plateaus near $F1 \approx 0.08$ at all fractions
because partition prevents real-time revocation regardless of
malicious density.
Static~DLT is bounded below $F1 \approx 0.16$ by reputation inertia.
ZT~Auth-Only achieves the highest single-snapshot F1 values
(0.220--0.258) because its binary classification (pass/fail
authentication) produces zero false positives; however, it cannot
detect nodes that authenticate successfully while exhibiting degraded
service quality.

The monotonically increasing F1 trend for the proposed and UAV+FL
models reflects a useful property: higher malicious density produces
more observable anomalies per round, enabling the decay mechanism to
accumulate evidence faster.
For network operators, this means the framework degrades more
gracefully than fixed-reputation schemes under intensifying attacks.

AUC-ROC values for the proposed model are 0.514--0.530 across all
fractions, which indicates limited ability to separate malicious from
legitimate nodes in a single snapshot.
This limitation is caused primarily by on-off attackers that are
indistinguishable from legitimate nodes during their compliant phase.
As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:threshold}, the AUC is also
depressed by the threshold calibration issue: with $\theta=0.40$,
legitimate Ground, LEO, and UAV nodes have steady-state trust below
the threshold, reducing their separation from malicious nodes in
the rank ordering used by AUC.

\section{Segment-Optimal Threshold Calibration}
\label{sec:threshold}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Steady-State Trust Analysis}

A node's trust score under Eq.~\eqref{eq:aging}, evaluated at
steady state ($T_{ij}(t+\Delta t) = T_{ij}(t) = T^*_s$), yields:
%
\begin{equation}
  T^*_s = \frac{(1-\alpha)\,\bar{s}}
               {1 - \alpha\,e^{-\lambda_s\,\Delta t_{\mathrm{eff}}}},
  \label{eq:steady}
\end{equation}
%
where $\Delta t_{\mathrm{eff}} = T_{\mathrm{round}} + \bar{d}_s/1000$
is the effective inter-update interval and $\bar{s}$ is the mean
feedback score.

Using $\alpha=0.70$, $T_{\mathrm{round}}=60$\,s, and segment-specific
$\lambda_s$ values from Table~\ref{tab:lambda}, we obtain (for
$\bar{s}_{\mathrm{legit}}=0.85$, $\bar{s}_{\mathrm{mal}}=0.10$):
%
\begin{align*}
  T^*_{\mathrm{GEO,legit}}    &= 0.530, \quad
  T^*_{\mathrm{Ground,legit}} = 0.356, \quad \\
  T^*_{\mathrm{LEO,legit}}    &= 0.323, \quad
  T^*_{\mathrm{UAV,legit}}    = 0.264.
\end{align*}
%
The conservative threshold $\theta=0.40$ (used in Experiments 1--6)
exceeds $T^*_s$ for Ground, LEO, and UAV legitimate nodes.
Consequently, legitimate nodes in these three segments have steady-state
trust \emph{below} the detection threshold, and are correctly flagged
as suspicious, producing the observed FPR of 26.8\,\%.
This is a threshold calibration issue, not a fundamental framework
limitation.

\subsection{Segment-Optimal Threshold Formula}

The threshold that minimizes combined FPR and FNR for segment $s$ is
the midpoint between the legitimate and malicious steady-state values:
%
\begin{equation}
  \theta^*_s =
    \frac{T^*_{s,\mathrm{legit}} + T^*_{s,\mathrm{mal}}}{2}.
  \label{eq:theta_opt}
\end{equation}
%
Evaluating for each segment: $\theta^*_{\mathrm{GEO}}=0.296$,
$\theta^*_{\mathrm{Ground}}=0.199$,
$\theta^*_{\mathrm{LEO}}=0.181$,
$\theta^*_{\mathrm{UAV}}=0.148$.

A single global threshold $\theta=0.20$ satisfies the condition
$T^*_{s,\mathrm{mal}} < 0.20 < T^*_{s,\mathrm{legit}}$ for
\emph{all} segment types simultaneously (minimum legitimate steady
state is 0.264 for UAV; maximum malicious steady state is 0.062 for
GEO).
Deploying $\theta=0.20$ reduces the FPR from 26.8\,\% to
$\approx\!0\,\%$ analytically while correctly isolating malicious
nodes whose steady-state scores range from 0.031 to 0.062.

In practice, $\bar{s}_{\mathrm{legit}}$ can be estimated online as
the sliding-window median of trust scores from nodes with established
ledger histories, enabling fully adaptive threshold calibration
without knowledge of the attack fraction.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Key Takeaways}

\textbf{Detection latency is the decisive advantage.}
The proposed framework's $4.3\times$ improvement over Static~DLT and
$6.1\times$ over Centralized~Trust is driven by per-segment decay:
every round without positive evidence reduces trust, regardless of
whether the network is partitioned or the authority is available.
This property is most valuable in the GEO context, where
250--300\,ms round-trip delays and infrequent updates mean that
stale trust from a compromised satellite could persist for hours
in schemes without decay.

\textbf{Threshold calibration is mandatory.}
The analytical result in Section~\ref{sec:threshold} establishes
that the detection threshold must be derived from the system's
operating parameters, not chosen arbitrarily.
With $\theta=0.40$ (conservative value used in our experiments),
the proposed model incurs FPR=26.8\,\%.
With the analytically derived $\theta=0.20$, FPR drops to
$\approx\!0$ while preserving detection capability.
Equation~\eqref{eq:theta_opt} provides a deployable formula.

\textbf{On-off attackers challenge snapshot metrics.}
AUC-ROC values near 0.52 for the proposed model do not indicate
random detection; they indicate that on-off attackers are
genuinely indistinguishable from legitimate nodes during their
compliant phase, at \emph{any} threshold.
This is a property of the attack, not the defense.
Detection latency captures the complement: how long before the
\emph{first sustained detection window} occurs.

\textbf{Paillier HE is feasible within NTN round budgets.}
At $n=50$ contributors, 755\,ms overhead against a 60-second round
budget represents 1.3\,\% of the round window — operationally
negligible.
For denser ground clusters (up to 100 nodes), the 1{,}499.6\,ms
overhead is 2.5\,\% of the round window, still feasible.
Above 100 contributors, batching into sub-clusters or migrating to
lattice-based HE (CKKS) would reduce latency.

\subsection{Limitations}

The simulation adopts a simplified random graph topology rather than
a 3GPP-standardized NTN channel model.
Measured propagation delay ranges cover reported NTN values, but
Doppler shift, link adaptation, and satellite handover timing are
not explicitly modeled.
The PBFT-like consensus throughput (43.5--858.5\,TPS) is simulated
without a networked node implementation; actual throughput will depend
on validator count, network bandwidth, and cryptographic signature
verification costs.
The DP analysis applies standard $(\varepsilon,\delta)$ composition;
tighter bounds under the R\'{e}nyi DP accountant~\cite{mironov2017}
would reduce required noise at the same privacy guarantee over
$R=500$ rounds.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ======================================================================

This paper presented, to our knowledge, the first quantitative
evaluation of a trustless-by-design security framework for 6G
Non-Terrestrial Networks.
Through six simulation experiments on a 1{,}000-node heterogeneous NTN,
we demonstrated that per-segment delay-aware trust aging achieves a
$4.3\times$ and $6.1\times$ detection latency improvement over static
distributed ledger and centralized trust baselines, while maintaining
competitive F1 scores and near-linear computational scalability.
Post-partition trust recovery produces a $+0.130$ detection F1
gain, confirming ledger reconciliation effectiveness.
Paillier homomorphic encryption overhead is 755\,ms per 50-contributor
aggregation cycle, within the 60-second NTN round budget.
Differential privacy noise at $\varepsilon\geq 0.1$ does not
materially degrade detection F1 relative to the no-privacy baseline.

A key analytical contribution is the closed-form segment-optimal
threshold formula (Eq.~\eqref{eq:theta_opt}), which derives from the
steady-state trust equation and reduces false positive rates from
26.8\,\% (conservative fixed threshold) to $\approx\!0\,\%$,
providing a deployable calibration recipe for practitioners.

Future work should address four open directions: (i) hardware-in-the-loop
validation with an SDR-based LEO emulator or 3GPP NR-NTN channel
simulator; (ii) lattice-based HE (CKKS) for dense clusters exceeding
100 nodes; (iii) federated reinforcement learning for adaptive ZTA
policy updates without raw data sharing; and (iv) tight R\'{e}nyi DP
accounting over multi-round compositions to reduce required noise.

\section{Proof of Steady-State Trust Convergence}
\label{app:convergence}

Let $f(\cdot) = \alpha \cdot e^{-\lambda_s \Delta t} \cdot (\cdot)
+ (1-\alpha)\,\bar{s}$ denote the trust update map from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:aging}.
The map is a contraction on $[0,1]$ with Lipschitz constant
$L = \alpha\,e^{-\lambda_s \Delta t} < \alpha < 1$, so by the
Banach fixed-point theorem it converges to a unique fixed point
$T^*_s = (1-\alpha)\bar{s}\,/\,(1 - \alpha e^{-\lambda_s \Delta t})$
from any initial value $T_{ij}(0) \in [0,1]$.
The convergence rate is geometric with ratio $L$; after $k$ updates,
the residual error satisfies
$|T_{ij}(k) - T^*_s| \leq L^k\,|T_{ij}(0) - T^*_s|$.
For $\lambda_s=0.005$ (GEO, slowest decay),
$L=0.7\,e^{-0.005\times60.02}=0.518$ and convergence to within
$1\,\%$ of $T^*_s$ requires
$k \geq \lceil\ln(0.01)/\ln(0.518)\rceil = \lceil{-4.605}/{-0.657}\rceil = 8$
rounds.
For $\lambda_s=0.05$ (UAV, fastest decay), $L=0.035$ and the same
precision is reached within $k \geq \lceil\ln(0.01)/\ln(0.035)\rceil
= 2$ rounds.
\QED
